Codebook

Curated from prompts/deficit_language_analysis_spec.txt (the source of truth). Deficit framing locates the problem inside the student — treating difficulty as a stable property of the learner rather than a feature of the task, the moment, or the instruction. When in doubt, don't flag; a false positive is worse than a miss.

Flag when it fits one or more categories

AFixed-Ability Framing

Ability treated as innate/permanent, an unchangeable trait.

"she's just not a math person", "he's not a reader"
BDeficit Labeling / Grouping

Students named by what they lack; group identity defined by deficit.

"my low kids", "the struggling group", "the ones who can't read"
CProblem in Student / Home / Background

Locates the cause in the student or their background rather than instruction or task design.

"they come from homes where...", "these students don't have..."
DDeficit Attribution (Behavior/Motivation)

Assigns negative motives instead of examining instructional conditions.

"they just don't care", "he's lazy", "she's not trying"
ELowered Expectations

Cognitive demand reduced and justified by perceived inability.

"they can't handle complex problems, so I just give them facts"
FComparative Deficit

A student or class defined by falling short of another; worth measured by comparison.

"not as good as last year's class", "behind the advanced kids"
GTotalizing Negation

Sweeping "can't / doesn't understand anything" — all-or-nothing framing.

"this group can't do anything right", "she doesn't get any of it"
OtherOther (specify)

Clear deficit framing that doesn't fit A–G. Describe it in the "Other: specify" box and the note.

Use sparingly — prefer an A–G category if one fits.

Do not flag these

  • Neutral, specific error description — "that answer isn't right, let's check line 3" (names the error, not the child)
  • Factual formative assessment — "about half haven't got the inequality sign yet" (descriptive, not evaluative)
  • High-demand questioning / productive struggle — "try it before I help", "what do you notice?" (positions students as capable)
  • Encouragement / asset-based moves — "I know this is hard, and I see you working through it"
  • Ambiguous cases — prefer not flagging; if you do, mark confidence low and explain the tension in the note.

Confidence

high

Clear, unambiguous deficit framing; strong category match; explicit ability or motivation attribution.

"she's just not good at math"
medium

Deficit reading present but with some ambiguity; context supports it; more likely deficit than neutral. The default.

"they struggle with comprehension"
low

Genuinely ambiguous between deficit and neutral; needs charitable reading. Flag to discuss, not to assert — explain the tension.

"about half the class hasn't mastered this yet"
